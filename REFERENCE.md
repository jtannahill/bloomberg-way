## Chapter 1: The Bloomberg Way

Ten guiding principles that define the Bloomberg editorial standard:

1. **It isn't news if it isn't true.** Accuracy is non-negotiable. A story that is wrong about a small thing calls into question everything else.
2. **News is not a commodity.** The goal is not to match the competition but to own the story. Add value with data, context, and original reporting.
3. **We are defined by our words.** Precision and clarity in language are professional obligations, not stylistic preferences.
4. **Show, don't tell.** Facts, anecdotes, and examples — not characterizations — do the work. Let the evidence speak.
5. **News is a surprise.** The story is what changed. If nothing changed, there is no story.
6. **Names make news.** Identify the specific people involved. Anonymous collectives — "analysts," "traders," "officials" — weaken a story.
7. **Not invented here.** Credit competitors and other sources when they break news first. Transparency builds credibility.
8. **Follow the money.** Financial motive explains most human behavior. Trace it.
9. **One story for all.** The same story serves all Bloomberg platforms and all audiences, worldwide. Write for a universal reader.
10. **The more we prepare, the luckier we will be.** Templates, research, sourcing lists, and planned scenarios replace luck with readiness.

## Chapter 2: The Five Fs

Every Bloomberg story should be:

| F | Meaning |
|---|---------|
| **Factual** | Accurate in every detail, every number, every name |
| **First** | Break it before the competition |
| **Fastest** | Get it out without sacrificing accuracy |
| **Final** | The definitive version — comprehensive, corrected, complete |
| **Future** | Answer: what happens next? What does this mean going forward? |

## Chapter 3: The Four-Paragraph Lead

The standard structure for a Bloomberg news story:

### Paragraph 1 — Theme
- What happened and why it matters
- Establish direction (up/down/won/lost/agreed/refused)
- Include comparison (vs. estimates, vs. prior period, vs. peers)
- State size and scope
- No jargon; no vague openers like "In a move that could..." or "Amid concern..."

### Paragraph 2 — Details
- Supporting facts and statistics
- Location, time, attribution
- Named people with titles
- Specific numbers

### Paragraph 3 — Quotation
- A quote from the person with the most at stake — the decision-maker, CEO, or central authority
- Must be in plain English; no jargon inside quotes if avoidable
- Introduce speaker at the first convenient break, usually the end of the first sentence
- No spokespeople as lead quote sources unless unavoidable

### Paragraph 4 — Nut Paragraph
- What is at stake for the reader
- Forward-looking context
- "So what" for the audience

### "To Be Sure" Paragraphs
- Include 1–2 paragraphs presenting the counterargument or qualification
- Required for balance; prevents one-sided narrative

### Headlines
- Maximum 64 characters including spaces
- Lead with names (people or companies), not verbs
- Capture surprise, what's at stake, conflict, and resolution
- Use present tense
- Capitalize all words except 2–3 letter prepositions and conjunctions
- First four words carry the most weight — make them count
- Add a story subhead every full screen of text

## Chapter 4: Show, Don't Tell

The rule: **Never characterize. Always demonstrate.**

**Banned characterizations (require evidence instead):**
- "lavishly compensated" → say the number
- "hugely successful" → say the revenue, profit, or market share
- "robust economy" → cite the GDP growth rate
- "cautiously optimistic" → press for specifics
- "shareholder value" → say what the company intends to do to its share price

If you cannot show it with facts, anecdotes, or examples, do not write it.

## Chapter 5: Writing Well Matters

### Orwell's Six Rules (applied to financial journalism)
1. Never use a long word where a short one will do
2. Never use a foreign phrase, scientific word, or jargon if there is an everyday equivalent
3. If it's possible to cut a word, cut it
4. Never use the passive where the active will do
5. Never use a metaphor, simile, or other figure of speech that you've seen in print
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous

### Preferred Substitutions
| Fancy | Plain |
|-------|-------|
| commence | begin |
| deactivate | close / shut |
| announced | said |
| finalize | complete |
| utilize | use |
| prior to | before |
| in the event that | if |
| at this point in time | now |

### Core Writing Principles
- **Past tense + active voice** — standard for all news stories
- **850 words maximum** — most stories should be shorter
- **No jargon** without immediate explanation
- **No clichés** (see banned list in Chapter 20)
- **No word echoes** — avoid repeating the same word in close proximity
- **Familiar over fancy** — choose the word more readers know
- **Specific over abstract** — "fell 4.2 percent" beats "fell sharply"

## Chapter 6: Preparation

**The six elements of preparation:**

1. **Keep in touch** — maintain source relationships before news breaks
2. **Collect string** — gather background material continuously
3. **Examine data** — know the Bloomberg terminal functions relevant to your beat
4. **Make a plan** — have templates and multiple leads written before the event
5. **Know the top 10** — maintain a prioritized list of stories to develop on your beat
6. **Logistics** — know where the event is, who will be there, and how to transmit

### Templates
Write 3 templates in advance for any scheduled economic release:
- Beat forecasts
- Miss forecasts
- Match forecasts

## Chapter 7: Headlines

### Five Elements of a Good Headline
1. **Surprise** — captures the unexpected
2. **What's at stake** — why the reader should care
3. **Names** — specific people or companies
4. **Conflict** — what is being disputed or won/lost
5. **Resolution** — what was decided or determined

### Headline Rules
- **64 character maximum** (including spaces)
- Start with names, not verbs
- Use **present tense** (not "reported" — use "reports")
- Capitalize all words except short prepositions/conjunctions (2–3 letters)
- Capitalize the word after a hyphen: *State-Owned*, *E-Mail*, *Second-Largest*
- Do not start with a numeral; write it out or restructure
- **First four words** are most important — front-load the news
- Headlines and text numbers must match exactly

## Chapter 8: Covering News

### Breaking News Timeline
- **First story:** within 3 minutes, maximum 2 sentences
- **First update:** within 15 minutes with additional context
- Each subsequent update adds new information; never recycle without advancing

### Attribution Standards
- Be precise — name the person, document, or report
- Avoid: "sources said," "analysts said," "investors said"
- Every claim needs a named source or a verifiable document
- Attribute to the specific person, not a collective

### Anonymous Sources
- Minimum 2 people with direct knowledge of the situation
- No direct quotes from anonymous sources
- Describe why they cannot be named: "asked not to be identified because the matter is private"
- Do not say a source "declined to be named" — say they "asked not to be named"

### Corrections
- Use **(Correct)** in the headline
- Explain the correction in the trashline (first line below headline)
- Do not bury or hide corrections

## Chapter 9: People

- Stories about money are stories about people
- Name, age, and title are facts as important as any financial figure
- Age is a benchmark of life — include it when relevant
- Identify who has the most at stake; that is usually the most important source
- The person the story affects most should be the person most quoted

## Chapter 10: Enterprise

Enterprise reporting is original work that goes beyond official releases:

- Combine data with interviews to find the story no one else has written
- Mine Bloomberg terminal data for undiscovered patterns
- Build exclusive access through sustained source relationships
- Enterprise stories require: a clear theme, supporting data, named sources, and a "so what" for readers
- Predict what happens next — don't just report what happened

## Chapter 11: Ethics

Key ethical standards:

- **No plagiarism** — credit all sources; never present another outlet's work as original
- **No fabrication** — no invented quotes, composite characters, or made-up scenes
- **Selective disclosure (Reg FD)** — use NI HUSH function to monitor; if a company discloses material information to one outlet, that is a regulatory violation
- **Conflicts of interest** — disclose personal financial interests; do not hold positions in securities you cover
- **Legal exposure** — in some countries, writing that an executive was "fired" without documentary support can be defamation; be precise
- **Obscenities** — only in quotations, only when newsworthy, only with executive editor approval

## Chapter 12: How We Work

- One story serves all platforms — the original is optimized for everyone
- Updates must advance the story, not just recycle existing content
- Stories travel around the world; write for a reader who has no local context
- Apply standard American spelling, punctuation, and usage globally
- British/Australian spelling: acceptable only in proper names (Labour Party)
- Datelines: use current day in the city where the main news occurred

## Chapter 14: Five Easy Pieces

Every Bloomberg story — regardless of beat — should address all five dimensions (with appropriate emphasis):

| Piece | What It Covers |
|-------|---------------|
| **Economy** | Macroeconomic conditions, indicators, trends |
| **Markets** | How securities, currencies, and commodities react |
| **Government** | Policy, regulation, legislation, central bank action |
| **Industries** | Sector-wide implications |
| **Companies** | Specific company impact — winners and losers |

A market story that ignores the economic backdrop is incomplete. A company story that ignores the industry context is incomplete. Weave all five into every piece.

## Chapter 15: Markets

### How to Cover Markets — Core Questions Every Story Must Answer
1. What happened to investments?
2. Why did it happen?
3. How does it compare to past performance?
4. Who said what — and why do they matter?
5. How does this change investor behavior?
6. What does it mean for the future?

### Writing Market Leads
- Name which prices, yields, or rates moved — and why
- Avoid vague causal language: "amid concern" is not a reason
- Avoid "on the one hand / on the other hand" constructions
- Be specific about what made today different from yesterday

### The Four Pillars of Market Reporting
1. **The market** — the benchmark instrument and its benchmark questions
2. **The buy side** — mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds (named managers)
3. **The sell side** — securities firms and investment banks (named analysts)
4. **External influences** — politics, economic data, disasters, regulation, weather

### Verbs for Market Moves
| Up | Down |
|----|------|
| rose | fell |
| gained | dropped |
| advanced | lost |
| climbed | declined |
| rallied | slumped |
| surged | retreated |
| jumped | tumbled |

### Keeping Stories Fresh
- Look beyond the benchmark to explain the why
- Distinguish today from any other day — what is specifically new?
- Provide weekly context when useful
- Cover biggest movers by percentage, not just by name
- Connect dots between markets (bond yields moving → currency impact → equity reaction)

### Technical Analysis
- **RSI (Relative Strength Index):** below 30 may suggest further gains; above 70 may suggest pullback
- **Moving averages:** use `GP`, `TE`, `BOLL` on Bloomberg
- **Fibonacci charts:** use `GPF` — only cite when used by a named trader
- Frame as: "Traders who rely on chart patterns say..."

### Stocks
Primary themes: earnings reports, economic indicators, interest-rate changes, relative value comparisons

### Bonds — Government
- Lead with the benchmark 10-year yield
- Highlight the yield, not just price
- Provide comparisons (vs. week ago, vs. year ago)
- Show how yield changes affect everyday costs (mortgages, car loans)
- Themes: economic outlook changes, central bank policy, new supply, relative value, risk

### Corporate Bonds
- Focus on **spread** — the yield premium over the government benchmark
- Junk bonds = rated below BBB– (S&P) / Baa3 (Moody's)

### Swaps
- Interest-rate swaps, currency swaps, equity swaps, credit-default swaps
- $693 trillion outstanding globally at end of 2013
- Always define the type and explain the mechanism

### Money Markets
- Cover overnight rates, 1/3/6/12-month deposit rates, Libor

### Currencies
- Verbs: rise/fall, weaken/strengthen, appreciate/depreciate
- Do not use "weak" or "strong" as permanent labels — only when an official uses them
- Quote per market convention
- Calculate percentage change
- Show who wins and who loses from the move
- Themes: public statements, economic reports, political events, trade data, other markets

### Commodities
- Themes: global events, supply reports, supply/demand shifts, economic data, government actions, weather, seasonal patterns, regulation
- Express commodity prices as singular: "The price fell" not "Prices fell"

## Chapter 16: Companies

### Market Perspective
- Always include stock price in context: 1-year performance, peer comparisons, P/E ratio
- Always include a debt paragraph: spread, credit ratings, CDS levels
- During trading hours, use general terms ("fell about 4 percent") — not exact prices that will be stale
- After hours, use precise closing prices

### Earnings Coverage
- The story is the **surprise** — beats, misses, and in-line results measured against the street estimate
- Review analyst estimates before the release; write templates
- **First headlines:** compare profit and sales to estimates
- **Follow-up story:** dig into balance sheet and cash flow
- Always include **net income** (or **net loss**) — never only adjusted figures
- Be skeptical of: *pro forma*, *special*, *one-time*, *adjusted*, *cash earnings* — always specify what is being excluded
- **Language:** profits rose/increased/fell/decreased; losses widened/narrowed/deepened/shrank
- Include company and industry perspective; make it personal (CEO reaction, shareholder impact)
- Focus on after-tax figures

### Mergers & Acquisitions
- "Company A will **buy** Company B" — not "merge" unless equal partners
- **Value calculation:**
  - Cash deal: offer price × shares outstanding
  - Stock deal: exchange ratio × buyer share price × seller shares (and equivalents)
  - Combined: cash + stock components
  - **Enterprise value** = base acquisition price + assumed debt − cash
- Use conditional language until deal closes: "the combined company **would** be..."
- Weave investment bankers into the narrative — identify them by name and role
- **Value comparisons:** market price vs. offer price; what it implies about bid probability
- Ask: Is it cheap or expensive? What has the buyer acquired before? Can they afford this?

### Financing
5 questions to answer in the first 4 paragraphs of any securities sale story:
1. How much is being raised?
2. Why is the company raising it now?
3. What will they do with the proceeds?
4. Why do investors want to buy?
5. Will the transaction succeed?

### IPOs
- Include percentage of company being sold
- Compare P/E to industry peers
- Identify lead underwriters

### Bond Sales
- Debt load, interest expense, credit ratings, yield comparison, convertibility terms

### Repurchases & Dividends
- Spotlight percentage of shares being repurchased
- Cutting a dividend is a signal of distress — highlight clearly
- Provide both old and new dividend amounts

## Chapter 17: Economies

### Five Economic Elements
Every economy story should address (with varying emphasis):

1. **Inflation** — is it rising, falling, stable?
2. **Interest rates** — what central banks are doing and why
3. **Jobs** — employment levels, unemployment rate, wage growth
4. **Sales** — consumer spending, retail data
5. **Production** — manufacturing, industrial output

### Covering Economic Data
- Prepare 3 templates before every scheduled release (beat / miss / match)
- Write economy stories as **company stories writ large** — use CEOs, anecdotes, human examples
- Back up anecdotes with charts and data
- Use setup quotes (sourced before the release)
- Watch the **titles** — describe the indicator rather than just naming it
- In headlines: start with the **country name**
- Show month-to-month change, not just year-over-year
- Always include the **market's reaction** to the number
- Package as a daily roundup when multiple indicators are released

## Chapter 18: Government

### Core Questions for Any Policy Story
1. Which companies or industries win? Which lose?
2. What is the effect on revenue?
3. What is the effect on costs?
4. What is the effect on market share?
5. What does this mean for company/industry strategy?
6. Does this create acquisition or divestiture opportunities?
7. What is the probability of passage?
8. Who are the people developing the policy?

### Follow the Money
- Markets are the front line of reaction to government action
- Investors respond before journalists finish writing
- Include lobbyists in source lists — they know what is being shaped before it becomes public

### Opinion Polls
- Always include:
  - Sample size
  - Who was polled
  - Margin of error — always written as ±x.x percentage points
  - When conducted
  - Where and how
- Identify poll by **organization name**, not generic "a poll"
- Use "shows" not "predicts"
- Ignore click-in and call-in surveys entirely

## Chapter 19: Grammar

### Subject-Verb Agreement

**Collective nouns take singular verbs:**
- committee, staff, public, jury, group, team, panel, board, company names

**Always singular:**
- no one, nobody, anyone, everyone, someone, each

**Singular or plural depending on context:**
- none, some, any, all, most, more
  - Singular if replaceable with "a single part"
  - Plural if replaceable with "at least two"

### Commas
- Use after introductory elements
- Before a coordinating conjunction **only** if the second clause has its own subject and verb

### Hyphens
- Use for **compound modifiers before nouns:** *first-quarter profit*, *full-time job*, *12 million-unit purchase*
- Do **not** hyphenate after a noun: *she works full time*
- Hyphenate after the verb *to be*: *George is well-known*
- In headlines, capitalize the word after a hyphen: *State-Owned*, *E-Mail*

### "Only"
Place as close as possible to the word it modifies:
- "She spoke **only** to the analyst" (not to anyone else)
- "**Only** she spoke to the analyst" (no one else did)

### "Which" vs. "That"
- **Non-restrictive clause** (not essential to meaning) → *which* + comma
  - *The fund, which invests in bonds, returned 8 percent.*
- **Restrictive clause** (essential to meaning) → *that* without comma
  - *The fund that invests in bonds returned 8 percent.*

### "That" Retention
Retain *that* after verbs like: *assert*, *contend*, *declare*, *estimate*, *make clear*, *point out*, *propose*, *state*, *suggest* — omitting *that* may cause readers to misread the following words as a direct object.

- Wrong: *Shareholders contend growth is positive.*
- Right: *Shareholders contend **that** growth is positive.*

### Has and Have — Present Perfect
Use present perfect for:
- Events that began in the past and continue in the present
- Actions completed when the statement is made
- Actions that happen frequently or continuously

**Common errors:**

**Using present perfect instead of simple past for fresh news:**
- Wrong: *The board has proposed selling more shares.*
- Right: *The board proposed selling more shares.*

**Using simple past when present perfect is required:**
- Wrong: *The shares rose 2.6 percent since Jan. 15.*
- Right: *The shares have risen 2.6 percent since Jan. 15.*

**Using present perfect when a specific time would be stronger:**
- Weak: *The analyst has lowered his price target.*
- Better: *The analyst lowered his price target last week.*

### Who vs. Whom
- **Who** = subject (equivalent to *he*, *she*, *they*)
- **Whom** = object (equivalent to *him*, *her*, *them*)
- Test: substitute *he* or *him*. If *he* works → *who*. If *him* works → *whom*.
  - *Who is at bat?* → *He is at bat.* → ✓
  - *Whom did you speak to?* → *Did you speak to him?* → ✓ (whom)

## Chapter 20: Words and Terms

A reference glossary for financial news style. The primary authority is STYL <Go> on the Bloomberg terminal; the secondary authority is the *Associated Press Stylebook*; the spelling reference is *Webster's New World College Dictionary* (4th edition).

**General principle:** Apply standard American spelling, usage, and punctuation. Choose words that are understood globally.

---

### A

**about** — Use instead of *approximately* or *around* for inexact figures.

**above** — Refers to location. Use *more than* for amounts: *He received more than 51 percent of the votes.*

**academic degrees** — MBA (no periods). Other degrees take periods: B.A., M.S., Ph.D. Better yet, write the full phrase: *master's degree in marine biology.*

**accents** — Omit accent marks. German/Swedish umlauts: use vowel + e (ae, oe, ue). All other cases: write vowels without accents.

**acquisition** — Most transactions are acquisitions, not mergers. Use *acquisition*, *purchase*, or *takeover* on first reference. *Merger* is appropriate only when companies are essentially equal partners. M&A is acceptable on second reference and in headlines.

**admit** — Use as a synonym for *said* only in legal contexts (court testimony). *Admit* implies subjective judgment.

**advancers, decliners** — Jargon. Say how many stocks rose or fell.

**adviser** — Not *advisor*, except in a proper company name.

**age** — Include for any newsmaker when relevant. A benchmark of life, as important as any anecdote. Not needed for spokespeople or analysts commenting in a formal capacity.

**alleged, allegedly** — Use for unproven, unattributed assertions. Not needed when the statement is directly attributed to a person: *The accountant stole money, the prosecutor said.* Not needed when stating formal charges: *The accountant was charged with embezzlement.*

**almost, nearly** — *Almost* = amount or degree. *Nearly* = distance.

**American depositary receipts** — Not *depository*. ADR acceptable in headlines and on second reference.

**antitrust** — Not *anti-trust*.

**application** — Full word on first reference and in headlines. *App* acceptable on second reference.

**arbitrager** — Not *arbitrageur*.

**attribution** — Be precise. Avoid: *sources said*, *analysts said*, *investors said*. Name speakers and say why they matter. Attribute to one individual, not as representative of a group. Anonymous sources: say they *asked not to be named* and give the reason. Preferred verb: *said* (neutral). Avoid: *conceded*, *noted*, *announced*, *claimed*, *explained*, *exclaimed*, *remarked*, *observed*, *added*, *opined*, *defended*, *warned* — these imply bias. *Confirm* only when topic already introduced. Official statistics don't need attribution in headlines; statistics from nongovernment organizations do.

**average, mean, median** — *Average* = *mean*. *Median* = middle number. Use median when surveying analysts for consensus forecasts.

---

### B

**bankrupt** — Acceptable for companies in bankruptcy proceedings. Specify Chapter 7 (liquidation) or Chapter 11 (reorganization). *Emerge from bankruptcy* is trite — use *come out of*, *complete its bankruptcy reorganization*.

**bargain hunting** — Jargon. Give the price at which people may buy.

**basic earnings per share** — Use only when a company doesn't provide diluted EPS.

**basis point** — 0.01 percentage point. Use to describe yield, not price. Numerals for two-digit numbers: *75 basis points*, *4 1/2 basis points*; words for one-digit: *three basis points*, *one basis point*.

**Beige Book** — Capitalize both words. On first reference: the Federal Reserve's latest assessment of regional economies.

**believe** — Not a synonym for *say*. Journalists know only what people say and do.

**benchmark** — A standard by which others in a category can be measured. Always explain what is being benchmarked: *benchmark bond*, *benchmark stock index*. Don't use *benchmark* alone.

**big data** — Lowercase; do not quote or precede with *so-called*. Hyphenate as modifier: *big-data processing*.

**blue chips** — Jargon. Be specific: *one of the 30 stocks in the Dow Jones Industrial Average*.

**board of directors** — Use *board* or *directors* for U.S. companies. European companies may have a two-tier system: management board (runs business) + supervisory board (outside directors, strategic decisions).

**bond ratings:**

| Grade | S&P | Moody's |
|-------|-----|---------|
| Investment grade | AAA AA A BBB | Aaa Aa A Baa |
| Junk | BB B CCC CC C D | Ba B Caa Ca C |

- Don't use *upgraded* or *downgraded* — write: *Moody's cut its rating to Baa from A3.*
- Use *company*, not *agency*, for rating providers (they are corporations).

**book value** — Shareholders' equity; assets minus liabilities. Often stated per share.

**BRIC, BRICS** — BRIC = Brazil, Russia, India, China. BRICS adds South Africa. Both all capitals.

**British spelling** — Acceptable only in proper names: *Labour Party*.

**business model** — Jargon. Explain what the corporate strategy is.

**but** — Use only to signal an about-face. Avoid repeated use. *Although*, *despite*, *however* often confuse more than clarify — they take readers in two directions at once.

**buyback, buy back** — One word as noun; two words as verb.

**buy side, sell side** — Jargon; use only in a compelling quote. Buy side = mutual funds, hedge funds, PE funds, insurance companies. Sell side = brokers, dealers. Two words as nouns; hyphenate as modifiers: *sell-side advice*.

---

### C

**capital controls** — Government-imposed limits on money flow. Be specific: *The government barred anyone from taking its currency out of the country.*

**capitals** — Cities, not governments. Washington is not the American people; Moscow is not the Putin government.

**carry trade** — Define in stories. Borrow in low-rate currency, convert, lend at higher rate. Profit = spread between rates; risk = exchange rate changes.

**cash earnings** — Avoid. Also known as *pro-forma earnings*. Specify what is excluded.

**cash flow** — Define. Several variations: from operations, from investing, free cash flow, Ebitda. Cash flow and Ebitda are not the same.

**cash reserves** — Colloquial. Say exactly what the company has on hand.

**cautious** — Be more specific; describe the action or behavior.

**cautiously optimistic** — Overused. Press for specifics. Give the reader enough to decide whether the situation calls for optimism or caution.

**chairman, chairwoman** — Not *chairperson*. A female chairman is referred to as *chairman* unless she prefers *chairwoman*. If both chairman and CEO, provide both titles. In Europe, *chairman* = head of supervisory board; *chief executive officer* = head of management board.

**charge** — In corporate earnings: reduces profit; not an official accounting term. Use: *cost*, *expense*, *writedown*, *investment loss*, *severance pay*, *loss from an asset sale*.

**chief executive officer** — *Chief executive officer* on first reference. *Chief executive* or *CEO* on second. CEO acceptable in headlines.

**cities that stand alone (no country/state needed):**
Abu Dhabi, Amsterdam, Athens, Atlanta, Auckland, Baghdad, Baltimore, Bangkok, Barcelona, Beijing, Berlin, Bonn, Bogota, Boston, Brasilia, Brussels, Bucharest, Budapest, Buenos Aires, Cairo, Calgary, Cape Town, Caracas, Chicago, Cincinnati, Cleveland, Copenhagen, Dallas, Damascus, Denver, Detroit, Doha, Dubai, Dublin, Edinburgh, Frankfurt, Geneva, Gibraltar, Guatemala City, The Hague, Hamburg, Havana, Helsinki, Hong Kong, Honolulu, Houston, Indianapolis, Istanbul, Jakarta, Jerusalem, Johannesburg, Kolkata, Kuala Lumpur, Kuwait City, Las Vegas, Lima, Lisbon, London, Los Angeles, Luxembourg, Madrid, Manila, Marseille, Melbourne, Mexico City, Miami, Milan, Milwaukee, Minneapolis, Monaco, Montreal, Moscow, Mumbai, Munich, New Delhi, New Orleans, New York, Oklahoma City, Oslo, Ottawa, Panama City, Paris, Philadelphia, Phoenix, Pittsburgh, Prague, Quebec City, Rio de Janeiro, Rome, Rotterdam, St. Louis, Salt Lake City, San Antonio, San Diego, San Francisco, San Marino, Santiago, Sao Paulo, Seattle, Seoul, Shanghai, Singapore, Stockholm, Sydney, Taipei, Tehran, Tel Aviv, Tokyo, Toronto, Vancouver, Vatican City, Vienna, Warsaw, Washington, Zurich.

**claim** — Use only in a legal context involving an allegation.

**clichés — banned:**
*against the backdrop, at this point in time, barring major news, big business, breath of fresh air, campaign trail, claimed the life of, climbed the corporate ladder, court of public opinion, deer in the headlights, financial circles, hit the ground running, in the wake of, in this day and age, laid to rest, navel gazing, no holds barred, no uncertain terms, perfect storm, poster child, powers that be, pushing the envelope, reaping the benefits, ruffled feathers, stretched too thin, take the helm, trial by fire, unanswered questions, under fire, wake-up call, war-torn country, win-win situation, what a difference a year makes.*

**closely held** — Company controlled by a few people. Preferable to *privately held*. Not hyphenated.

**collateralized debt obligations** — CDO acceptable on second reference and in headlines. Define.

**company names** — Use full name on first reference. Shorten to one or two words in headlines and on second reference. Rules:
- Use ampersand (&) rather than *and* in company names that use it formally
- Capitalize first letter of names that begin with lowercase: *EBay* not *eBay*; *EasyJet* not *easyJet*
- If more than 3 letters in a row are capitalized and the name doesn't have to be spelled out: *ABN Amro* not *ABN AMRO*
- Don't start with *The*: *Home Depot Inc.* not *The Home Depot Inc.*
- For companies with .com in the name: include on first reference (*Amazon.com*); use shortened form in headlines (*Amazon*)
- Accepted designations: AB, AG, AS, Bancorp, Bhd., BV, CA, Cia., Cie., Co., Cos., Corp., GmbH, Inc., KGaA, Ltd., LLC, LLP, LP, NV, Oy, Oyj, Pcl, Plc, Pte, Pty, SA, Saca, Sdn., SpA

**compared to, compared with** — *Compared to*: similarities between essentially different things. *Compared with*: differences between essentially similar things (earnings comparisons).

**concern about, concern that** — Follow *concern* with *that*, not *about*. Identify who is concerned and explain specifically what they fear.

**conglomerate** — Name the businesses instead. Don't use with an industry (*financial conglomerate*).

**consensus** — Not a synonym for *average* or *mean* in earnings estimates.

**consortium** — Use *group*.

**consultancy** — Use *consulting firm* or *consultants*.

**continue** — What continues is not news. Better: use a historical comparison. *The economy grew for the sixth straight quarter* beats *The economy continued to grow.*

**convexity** — A measure of the change in duration as yields change. Explain in stories.

**convince, persuade** — *Convince* involves beliefs. *Persuade* involves actions. *Convinced of*, *convinced that*; *persuaded to*.

**core** — Jargon when describing corporate strategy. Describe the actual business or asset.

**correction** — Jargon in market stories. Prices aren't mistakes.

**credit-default swaps** — Contracts based on bonds and loans; used to speculate on or hedge against default risk. Give prices in basis points. Always include a definition.

**currencies** — Use currency of denomination in all references. After first non-U.S. dollar reference, provide dollar conversion in parentheses: *Sales increased to 400 million euros ($543 million).* Rounding: nearest million for amounts under 1 billion; one decimal place for 1 billion or more. Currency abbreviations: A$ (Australia), C$ (Canada), HK$ (Hong Kong), NZ$ (New Zealand), S$ (Singapore), NT$ (Taiwan). European abbreviations in headlines only: EU (euro), CHF (Swiss franc), DK (Danish krone), NK (Norwegian krone), SK (Swedish krona). Don't use currency symbols for yen, euro, British pound — write the amount in full: *12.7 billion rupees*, *500 million pounds*.

**currency band** — Range within which a government allows its currency to fluctuate.

**currency peg** — Link to another currency or fixed exchange rate. Explain the mechanism.

**currency swap** — Agreement to exchange periodic interest-rate payments in different currencies. Define.

### D

**damp, dampen** — *Damp* = cool or restrain (*damp earnings*). *Dampen* = moisten (*dampen the mop*).

**data** — When citing Bloomberg-compiled data: *according to data compiled by Bloomberg* (first reference); *according to Bloomberg data* (subsequent). When editors survey sources: *according to 14 analysts surveyed by Bloomberg News.*

**datelines** — Current day in the city where the main news occurred. Don't let a story cross midnight.

**deal** — Too vague for first reference. Use: *contract*, *sale*, *purchase*, *trade pact*, *loan*, *compromise*.

**debt** — In takeover context, use alone only when acquirer is exchanging bonds for shares. Use *assumed debt* when the target owes the money.

**deflation, disinflation** — *Deflation* = general price drop (severe slowdown). *Disinflation* = slowing inflation (often during recession).

**derivatives** — Contracts whose values derive from stocks, bonds, loans, currencies, commodities, or events. Two definitions:
1. "Derivatives are contracts whose values are derived from stocks, bonds, loans, currencies and commodities or linked to specific events such as changes in interest rates or the weather."
2. "Derivatives are financial instruments used to hedge risks or for speculation... Options and futures are the most common types."

**diluted earnings per share** — Reflects all securities that may become common shares. Use this number instead of basic EPS whenever possible.

**double-digit** — Be specific: *Sales rose 13 percent.* Press for the actual figure.

**downside** — Jargon for decline.

**Dragon bonds** — Debt securities denominated in U.S. dollars, sold in Asian countries outside Japan.

**duration** — A measure of how much a bond price will change when interest rates move. Longer maturity = greater duration = greater price sensitivity.

**Dutch auction** — Offer in which investors specify the price, within a set range. Define.

---

### E

**easing** — Central banks ease *credit* through policy changes. Better: *pushed interest rates lower.*

**Ebitda** — Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Write out on first reference. Ebitda is acceptable in headlines and on second reference. Not the same as cash flow.

**edge** — Reserve as a noun. *Rise* or *fall* are preferable for price moves. *Edged higher/lower* is imprecise.

**effect** — As a verb: to bring about. As a noun: a consequence. Don't confuse with *affect* (verb: to influence; noun: a feeling).

**estimate** — Analysts give *estimates* (rough calculations); companies supply *forecasts* (projections based on more complete knowledge).

**Euribor** — Euro interbank offered rate. Benchmark for euro money-market rates. Published by European Banking Federation.

**euro** — Single currency used by 18 countries. Never use the symbol. One euro = 100 cents.

**Eurobonds, euro bonds** — A Eurobond: corporate or sovereign debt issued in a different currency than the one where the borrower is based. A euro bond: debt issued in euros by borrowers within the euro zone.

**Eurodollars** — Dollars held in banks outside the U.S.

**European Central Bank** — Sets monetary policy for euro-zone countries. Based in Frankfurt.

**European Union** — 28 member states. EU acceptable on second reference. Not a *region* — a group or partnership.

**euro zone** — Countries using the euro. Alternatives: *countries that share the euro*, *euro region*, *euro area*. Hyphenate as adjective: *euro-zone economy*.

**ex-** — Place before the entire subject: *ex-UBS banker*, not *UBS ex-banker*.

**exchange rate** — Value of one currency in terms of another. Fixed or floating.

**exchange-traded funds** — ETF acceptable on second reference.

**expected** — News is a surprise; *expected* doesn't qualify. Use *plans*, *may*, or *will* when possible.

---

### F

**401(k)** — Retirement savings plan provided by some U.S. companies.

**factors** — Often used as a synonym for *reasons*, which is preferable.

**fall** — Not to be used as a noun to describe a change in value in a headline.

**farther, further** — *Farther* = distance. *Further* = extent.

**fear** — Presumptuous to call people fearful unless they say "I'm afraid" on the record.

**federal funds** — Money U.S. banks have on deposit at Federal Reserve banks. Fed funds rate = the Fed's interest-rate target for overnight loans between banks.

**Federal Open Market Committee** — Determines U.S. monetary policy. FOMC acceptable in headlines and on second reference.

**Federal Reserve** — U.S. central bank. *Federal Reserve* on first reference; *Fed* in headlines and on second reference. Write *governor* in full in all references.

**fewer, less** — *Fewer* = countable things. *Less* = measured but not counted things.

**finalize** — Don't use. Use *complete*.

**Finra** — Financial Industry Regulatory Authority. Oversees U.S. securities firms.

**fire** — Use *fired* or *dismissed* rather than *laid off* or *made redundant* when workers are permanently let go. Reserve *laid off* for workers who may be called back.

**firm** — Use for private equity, money management, accounting, law, or brokerage entities (partnerships). Don't use as a synonym for *company*.

**fiscal periods** — Specify last day of a fiscal period only when it doesn't correspond with the calendar quarter. Use *fiscal* before the period number: *fiscal third quarter*, not *third fiscal quarter*. Use *ended* when period is over; *ending* when it isn't.

**flat** — *Unchanged* or *little changed* is preferable.

**fold** — Suffix as multiplier: sevenfold = 7 times. Increase of 100% = doubled = twofold. Hyphenate when number is 10 or more: *10-fold*.

**forecast** — Companies supply forecasts; analysts give estimates.

**forego, forgo** — *Forego* = to go before. *Forgo* = to do without.

**foreign words** — Use only those with wide acceptance in American English. If unavoidable, put in quotation marks and attribute.

**futures** — Agreements to buy or sell assets at a set price and date. Traded on exchanges; standardized.

---

### G

**GAAP** — Generally accepted accounting principles (U.S.). Abbreviation acceptable on second reference.

**glitch** — Minor malfunction from a brief power surge. Use *breakdown* for most other situations.

**goodwill** — Amount exceeding fair value of net assets in an acquisition. Evaluated annually; written down if permanently impaired.

**greenback** — Acceptable on second reference for the U.S. dollar. Avoid overuse.

**gross margin** — Shows earnings after deducting direct costs, as a percentage of sales.

**group** — Not a synonym for *company* except as part of a proper name.

**Group of Seven / Eight / Ten / Twenty:**
- G-7: Canada, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, U.K., U.S.
- G-8: G-7 + Russia
- G-10: G-7 + Belgium, Netherlands, Sweden
- G-20: EU + 19 countries with largest industrialized and emerging economies

**guidance** — Specify the earnings forecasts that companies provide.

---

### H

**haircut** — Jargon; use only in quotes and explain. Can mean a discount applied to collateral, or losses imposed on creditors.

**halve** — *Fell 50 percent* is stronger than *halved*.

**hedge funds** — Private pools of capital whose managers participate substantially in profits from speculation on asset price movements.

**hedging** — Using financial instruments to guard against adverse swings in interest rates, currencies, or other securities.

**high-yield bonds** — *Junk bonds* is acceptable on first reference, followed immediately by: *high-yield, high-risk debt*; rated below BBB– by S&P and Baa3 by Moody's.

**hike** — Often used after *price* or *rate*. More precise words, such as *increase*, are preferable.

**hyphens** — See Chapter 19. Additional examples: *10-year period*, *12-day rally*, *25-basis-point increase*, *50-million-unit inventory*, *23 billion-euro purchase*. No hyphen with currency amounts and percentages: *$14 trillion industry*, *28 percent gain*.

---

### I

**if** — Means "in the event that." *Whether* suggests alternatives. Don't use *if* to suggest a hypothetical unless attributed to a source.

**inflation** — Rising prices. *Rising inflation* is redundant.

**initial public offering** — IPO acceptable in headlines and on second reference. Write *initial public offering* not *initial public offer*.

**insiders** — Officers, directors, and major shareholders. Always provide the definition.

**interbank rates** — Rates at which banks lend to each other. Use *offered rates*, not *offer rates*. Key rates: Libor (London), Euribor (euro zone), Tibor (Tokyo), Sibor (Singapore).

**interest-rate swap** — Exchange of fixed-rate for floating-rate interest payments on a set amount for a set time. Define.

**intervention** — Government currency-market action. Explain: central banks *intervene* when they buy or sell currencies to influence exchange rates.

---

### J

**jargon** — Fields rife with jargon: business, finance, economics, science, technology. Translate every term. If unavoidable, define immediately: *choppy trade*, *guidance*, *to the upside*, *price target*.

**jitters** — Who knows whether the latest event has people shaking in their seats? Use *concern*.

**junk bonds** — Acceptable on first reference with explanation: *high-yield, high-risk debt*; rated below BBB– by S&P and Baa3 by Moody's.

---

### L

**large-cap** — Abbreviation for *large capitalization*. Provide a range of values (e.g., *more than $10 billion*). Descriptions vary; always ask for a definition.

**launch** — Reserved for ships, spacecraft, and missiles. Companies *sell* securities; *start* services; *introduce* products.

**level** — Often extraneous. Rates are a *rate*, not a *level*. Use only when a superlative alone would be awkward.

**Libor** — London interbank offered rate. Benchmark for money-market rates.

**lift** — Not a substitute for *raise* or *increase*.

**likely** — Not an adverb substituting for *probably*. Acceptable as an adjective: *She is likely to vote for the bill.*

**little changed** — Acceptable, but look for a more specific way to tell the story.

**loonie** — Acceptable on second reference for the Canadian dollar.

**loss** — Losses widen and narrow; they don't rise or fall.

---

### M

**-maker** — One-syllable words can take *maker* as suffix for established trades: *drugmaker*, *steelmaker*, *glassmaker*. Keep *maker* as separate word otherwise: *aircraft maker*, *computer-chip maker*.

**management board** — One of two tiers in some European company structures. Runs the business.

**margin** — Measure of profitability. Margins *widen* or *narrow*, not rise or fall. Be specific: gross margin, operating margin, or profit margin.

**mean, median, average** — *Average* = *mean*. Use *median* for survey consensus data.

**measurements** — Use the system of the country in the story; provide conversion on first reference.

**mid-cap** — Abbreviation for *mid-capitalization*. Provide a range: e.g., *$1 billion to $10 billion*.

**mixed** — Use only when benchmark measures differ substantially. *Little changed* is better for modest divergence. Leads that say prices *were mixed* aren't compelling.

**modifiers** — Only those derived from specific details are justified. *A more-than-expected 0.2 percent rise* is better than *a modest 0.2 percent rise* — the first is precise, the second is a judgment.

**money manager** — Preferable to *portfolio manager*.

**multimillion, multibillion** — No hyphen immediately after *multi*: *multimillion-dollar contracts*, *multimillionaire*.

---

### N

**names** — Provide full names of companies, organizations, and people on first reference. Consider personal preferences for names (e.g., *Bill Clinton* preferred to *William Jefferson Clinton*). For non-English names, follow the person's preference or local convention.

**narrow** — Losses, deficits, and margins do this when they get smaller.

**negative equity** — When liabilities exceed assets.

**net income, net loss** — The bottom line in earnings. Always required. Avoid *net earnings* or *net profit*.

**net interest income** — Interest received on loans and investments minus interest paid on deposits.

**net interest margin** — Spread a bank makes on loans vs. borrowings, as a percentage of assets.

**news reports** — Inadequate for attribution. Name the source specifically.

**noncore** — Jargon. Describe the actual business or assets.

**noninterest income** — Banks' income from fees, service charges, trading, and security sales.

**numbers:**
- Write *one through nine* as words unless before a unit of measurement, monetary unit, or age: *two-year note*, *8 euros*, *9-year-old boy*
- Numerals for *10 and above*
- Write *first through ninth*; numerals for *10th and above*
- Use commas in all numbers of 1,000 or more: *1,474.21*
- Use numerals for ages: *5-year-old boy*, *32 years old*
- Headlines and sentences should not begin with numerals (exception: names like *3M Co.*)
- For historic levels: don't round up. You're not 30 until you reach that exact date
- For ranges: *$10 million to $15 million* in text; in headlines, use *More Than $10 Million* or *Up to $15 Million*
- Round to two decimal places for numbers less than 10 million or between 1 billion and 10 billion: *9.23 million*, *6.14 billion*
- Round to one decimal place for 10 million to 1 billion, or more than 10 billion: *324.8 million*, *33.7 billion*

---

### O

**Obacare** — Acceptable on first reference and in headlines. Full name: the Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act of 2010.

**obscenities, profanities, vulgarities** — Only in quotations, only in a newsworthy context, only with executive editor approval. Write with hyphens substituting for letters after the first.

**ongoing** — Acceptable only in quotes.

**operating margin** — Earnings after all production, sales, admin, and accounting expenses. Expressed as percentage of sales.

**operating profit, operating income, operating loss** — Defined differently by different companies and industries. Define each time.

**options** — Contracts granting the right (not obligation) to buy or sell at a set price. Call options = right to buy; put options = right to sell. American-style: exercisable any time before expiry. European-style: only at expiry. *In the money* when underlying exceeds call strike; *out of the money* when below. Define on first reference.

**outage** — Use *failure* for power failures.

**outlook** — A company increases or reduces its earnings forecast. That means its outlook improved or worsened. The company didn't "increase its outlook."

**overbought, oversold** — Jargon. Better: write that a security is *poised to fall* or *poised to rise*, and explain the indicators.

---

### P

**partner** — Use as a verb only for ballet. Say *teamed*, *joined*, or *formed a partnership*.

**passthrough** — Type of mortgage-backed security. Define.

**percentages:**
- *Percent* is not the same as *percentage point*
- Express as numerals; for numbers less than 1: *0.9 percent*, not *.9 percent*
- Round under 10 to one decimal place: *9.7 percent*, not *9.68 percent*
- Round 10 and above to whole numbers: *15 percent*, not *15.3 percent*
- Don't hyphenate in compound modifiers: *14 percent gain*, not *14-percent gain*
- For near-100% or higher, use descriptive language: *more than doubled* rather than *rose 109 percent*

**poison pill** — Takeover defense giving shareholders the right to buy new stock at a discount. Introduce with *so-called* on first reference.

**portfolio** — Jargon for investments. Describe the actual holdings.

**possessives:**
- Singular nouns → add *'s*: *Xilinix's earnings*
- Singular nouns ending in *s* → add *'s*: *the boss's decision*, *Sears's sales*
- Classical names ending in *s* (unaccented final syllable) → add only apostrophe: *Moses' staff*
- Plural nouns ending in *s* → add only apostrophe: *the accountants' review*, *the CEOs' compensation*
- Plural nouns not ending in *s* → add *'s*: *the women's achievements*
- Joint possession → punctuate last word only: *Obama and Boehner's agreement*
- Individual possession → punctuate both: *Germany's and France's budgets*

**preferred** — Write *preferred shares* not *preference shares* or *preferential shares*.

**premium** — Price exceeding face value. Be specific when using this term.

**prepositions** — Use *below*, *under*, *above*, *over* only for spatial relationships. Don't use as substitutes for *less than* or *more than*.

**pretax** — Not *pre-tax*. Use only when after-tax figures aren't available; always label.

**price-earnings ratio** — Share price divided by EPS. Define in all cases. Avoid *price-to-earnings*.

**price target** — Jargon. Give the price and the date or period.

**private equity** — Explain exactly what the firm does: *leveraged buyouts*, *growth equity*, etc.

**private placement** — Securities sale to a small number of investors; not registered with SEC.

**privately held** — In many countries, means state-owned company, not investor-owned. For companies whose shares aren't traded: use *closely held*.

**privatization** — Use *the sale of state-owned companies* or equivalent.

**problem** — Specify. *The country's economic problems* is not precise. *The country's inability to tame inflation* is.

**profit margin** — Percentage of sales earned after all costs. Three types:
- *Gross margin*: after production costs
- *Operating margin*: after production, sales, marketing, admin, R&D
- *Net margin*: based on net income

**profit-taking** — Avoid. It's usually impossible to know if investors are making a profit or a loss.

**profit warning** — Avoid. Better: *The company lowered its forecast for third-quarter earnings.*

**pro forma** — Financial statements on a what-if basis, often with nonrecurring items excluded. Explain what is being measured.

**program trading** — Use *computer-guided trading* or *computer-driven trading*.

---

### Q

**quiet period** — SEC restrictions on what companies seeking to issue securities can discuss. Note that restrictions are less severe than companies often claim.

**quotations:**
- Direct quotes must convey exactly what was said
- Don't tinker with words; don't correct grammar if correction makes speaker look foolish — use an accurate paraphrase instead
- All quotations need named attribution
- Skip throat-clearing openings: *I think*, *Of course*, *As you know*, *In effect*, *Indeed*
- Start quotations at the beginning of paragraphs, not buried mid-paragraph
- Introduce speaker at first convenient break (end of first sentence); don't make readers search for the attribution
- Place commas and periods before closing quotation marks; colons and semicolons after
- Don't use ellipses to link partial sentences; use partial quotes or paraphrase
- If quoting something not obtained today, indicate when it was said
- Quotes should support the theme of the lead; come from a person of authority; spokespeople rarely make good lead quotes

---

### R

**raise** — For collecting/lifting items, not increasing: *The company increased the amount it plans to raise through bond sales.*

**rally** — A short, steep rise after downward or sideways movement. A rally can *end* but not *stall*.

**rankings** — Use *No. 1*, not *Number One*. When naming a top-3 ranking, cite the other two as well. Include the measure used to establish the ranking (sales, market cap, etc.).

**rate** — Always specify the type: *interest rate*, *exchange rate*, *inflation rate*.

**realized** — Gains or losses on investment sales. Differs from *unrealized* (based on value changes only).

**rebound** — Springing back from a bad situation. Specify: rebounded from when? Be precise about time element.

**recession** — In most countries: two consecutive quarters of falling GDP. In the U.S.: determined by the National Bureau of Economic Research.

**record** — *New record* and *all-time record* are redundant. Use: *record*, *all-time high*, *all-time low*.

**reform** — One person's reform is another's step backward. No way to know who is right; unwise to make the judgment.

**refute** — Not a synonym for *dispute*, *deny*, or *rebut*. To refute is to disprove.

**reorganization** — Obscures more than clarifies. Be precise: *said it will shut two of its six divisions and merge three others.*

**restructuring** — Hackneyed; can mean almost anything. Be specific: *will fire 2,000 mechanics, or 5 percent of its employees, close all of its plants and become a distributor.*

**rounding** — See *numbers* for the full rules. Key rules:
- Round to two decimal places for amounts under 10 million
- Round to one decimal place for 10 million to 10 billion
- Percentages under 10: one decimal place
- Percentages 10 and over: whole numbers
- Don't round a number differently in a headline than in the text

**rumor** — Don't report rumor or hearsay — it bestows undeserved credibility. Acceptable to report *speculation* (when traders buy or sell on unverifiable information). Don't use *speculation* for *prediction*, *expectation*, or *report*.

---

### S

**said** — The preferred attribution verb. Neutral. Substitutes (*conceded*, *noted*, *claimed*, *explained*, *remarked*, etc.) can imply bias. *Admitted*: legal stories only. *Confirm*: only when topic already established. *Acknowledge* and *deny* are acceptable when used appropriately.

**sales** — Preferable to *revenue* for companies based on selling goods and/or services.

**Samurai bonds** — Debt securities sold in Japan by foreign governments and companies.

**scheme** — Don't use to mean *plan* or *proposal* in American English — it implies illegality.

**seasons** — Avoid in time references: *completed in the fall* → *completed by December* or *by the fourth quarter*.

**sector** — Not a synonym for *industry*, *group*, or *segment*. Overuse makes it jargon.

**seem** — Report what is said and done, not what seems. What seems to one person may not to another.

**selloff** — Acceptable when a trigger event sparks selling. Spell without a hyphen.

**shareholder value** — Largely meaningless. Say what the company intends to do: boost share price, increase returns.

**shed** — Stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities don't shed. Use *fell*, *dropped*, *declined*, *tumbled*.

**short sale** — Sale of borrowed stock. Define in all cases.

**short squeeze** — Situation where short sellers must buy back shares because the price rose. Define.

**single-digit** — Be specific. A single-digit change ranges from 1 percent to 9 percent.

**slash** — Not a synonym for *reduce*. Means to cut with sweeping force. Better to give the specific reduction.

**small-cap** — Small capitalization. Provide a range: e.g., *$250 million to $1 billion*.

**so-called** — Use sparingly, for popularly used but unofficial terms. Don't combine with quotation marks.

**space** — Not a synonym for *industry*, *group*, *niche*, *market*, or *business*.

**spinoff** — *Spinoff* as noun; *spin off* as verb. Use only when a public company gives stock in a unit to shareholders. If selling the stock: use *sale*.

**spokesman, spokeswoman** — Use *spokesmen* for mixed-gender groups. Don't use *spokesperson* or *spokespeople*.

**sports metaphors** — Avoid; a worldwide audience won't understand them.

**spread** — In bonds: extra yield investors demand over the benchmark (usually Treasuries). Widening spread = more risk perceived; narrowing = less. Avoid using *spread* on first reference without definition.

**stock splits** — Use the new number of shares first. Always use numerals and hyphens: *3-for-2 stock split*.

**strategic alternatives** — Jargon. Describe what the alternatives actually are.

**strong, weak** — Use to describe currencies, commodities, economies, or governments only when an official or organization uses the term. Otherwise be specific.

**subsidiary** — Separately incorporated business within a parent. Acceptable alternative to *unit*.

**supervisory board** — One of two tiers in European company structures. Consists of outside directors; makes strategic decisions.

**swaps** — Specify type: credit-default, currency, interest-rate, etc. Weave definition into narrative.

---

### T

**tap** — Acceptable as synonym for *borrow* (not for hiring/personnel moves).

**technical analysis** — Market analysis based on historical price patterns. Better: *Some traders who rely on chart patterns expect the rally to end at about 15,800 on the Dow Jones Industrial Average.*

**temperatures** — Use Celsius or Fahrenheit for the country where the story takes place; provide conversion on first reference.

**tender, tender offer** — *Tender* = shareholder action (tendering shares). *Tender offer* = would-be purchaser's offer in an acquisition.

**territory** — There is no such place as *negative territory* or *positive territory*. Prices are higher or lower.

**terrorism, terrorist** — *Terrorism* = violent actions intended to intimidate for political reasons. When evidence isn't readily available, qualify assertions with *alleged* or *suspected*. Reserve *terror* for the emotion of intense fear.

**think tank** — Describe provenance and agenda; don't apply political labels. Provide self-stated mission. Include funding when available.

**Tibor** — Tokyo interbank offered rate.

**tightening** — Central banks tighten *credit*, not *policy*. Better: *pushed interest rates higher.*

**time of day** — Use colon: *4:30 p.m.*, not *4.30 p.m.* Omit 00 for whole hours: *4 p.m.*, not *4:00 p.m.*

**time zones** — Use major city or region: *5 p.m. Zurich time*, *midnight U.S. West Coast time*. Avoid abbreviations like EDT.

**titles:**
- Courtesy titles (Mr., Mrs., Miss, Ms.) are unnecessary
- True titles belong to elected officials and corporate/military officers
- Capitalize when preceding a name: *Executive Vice President John Smith*
- Lowercase when used as description: *John Smith, executive vice president*
- False titles (job descriptions) always go *after* the name: *Ruben Pascuali, an analyst at Mayoral Bursatil*
- Long titles always go after the name
- Titles of nobility/honorifics: don't use on first reference; use the name in standard form

**tracking stock** — Class of shares whose value reflects the performance of one unit, not the entire company.

**tranche** — Part of a stock or bond sale or bank loan. Use *portion* and define.

**troubled** — Show, don't tell. Supply evidence that the company is near insolvency.

**turnover** — Not a substitute for *sales* or *revenue*.

**tweet** — Acceptable as noun and verb for Twitter communications.

---

### U

**uncertain** — Describe the situation instead.

**undersubscribed** — Give specific amounts; avoid this jargon.

**underweight** — Describes a market segment accounting for a smaller percentage of a portfolio than its benchmark index. Provide specifics instead.

**unnamed** — Not a synonym for *unidentified* in anonymous source stories. Everyone has a name.

**unprofitable** — Preferable to *money-losing*, *loss-making*.

**unrealized** — Describes changes in investment value. Differs from *realized* (based on actual sales).

**upcoming** — Don't use. Be specific about when events will take place.

**upside** — Jargon for increase in a security or market.

---

### V

**valuation** — Security's price relative to earnings, sales, or net worth. Describe the measures rather than using the word alone.

**versus** — Write in full in text and story headlines. Abbreviate as *v.* in citing lawsuits: *Roe v. Wade.* Use *VS* only in stand-alone flash headlines.

**visibility** — Market jargon for a company's ability to predict its own earnings. When *visibility is low*, they're not sure what figures will look like.

**volatility** — Measure of how much a price, index, or interest rate varied from its average. Express as annual percentage terms: *10-day volatility*, *30-day volatility*.

**Volcker Rule** — Capitalize both words. Restricts types of risky trades deposit-taking banks may make with their own money. Part of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act.

**volume** — Be precise: *The stock rose 10 percent in trading of 2.3 million shares.* Not *volume of 2.3 million*. *Trading volume* is redundant.

---

### W

**warning** — Often paired with *profit*. Don't write about a company's failure as if detached from it. Better: *The company reduced its forecasts for third-quarter sales and earnings.*

**Web** — World Wide Web; the Web; website; Web page; webcam; webcast; webmaster; Web log (blog acceptable on first reference).

**wholly owned** — Unnecessary. Describe ownership stake only when it's less than 100 percent.

**widen** — Losses, deficits, and margins do this when they get larger.

**worry, worried, worries** — Subjective. Use only when someone says "I'm worried" on the record.

**worth** — Not a synonym for *value*. Use *valued at*. *$10 million of engines* can shorten *$10 million worth of engines*.

**writedown** — *Writedown* as noun; *write down* as verb. Many occur when a company permanently reduces an asset's value.

---

### Y

**Yankee bonds** — Debt securities sold in the U.S. by foreign governments and companies.

**year earlier** — The best way to refer to the prior year's same quarter: *a year earlier*. Don't use *in the year-earlier quarter*, *a year ago*, *in the same quarter a year earlier*. *A year ago* is inaccurate for earnings because the earnings comparison is with a period that ended more than a year ago.

---

### Z

**zero-coupon bonds** — Securities that mature in more than one year and don't pay interest.

## Quick Reference Card

### The Lead Test
Does the first paragraph answer:
- [ ] What happened?
- [ ] To whom?
- [ ] By how much?
- [ ] Compared with what?
- [ ] Why does it matter?

### The Story Test
Does the story include all Five Easy Pieces?
- [ ] Economy
- [ ] Markets
- [ ] Government
- [ ] Industries
- [ ] Companies

### The Attribution Test
- [ ] Named person or document (not "sources said")
- [ ] Why does this person matter?
- [ ] Did I use *said* (not *noted*, *claimed*, *conceded*)?
- [ ] No direct quotes from anonymous sources?

### The Numbers Test
- [ ] Rounded correctly?
- [ ] Percentage vs. percentage point distinguished?
- [ ] Headline numbers match text numbers exactly?
- [ ] Net income included in earnings story?

### The Jargon Test
Every term on this list needs a definition or a replacement:
*guidance, upside, downside, visibility, overbought, oversold, cautiously optimistic, big business, blue chips, bargain hunting, restructuring, reorganization, strategic alternatives, space, sector, core, noncore, profit warning, shareholder value.*
